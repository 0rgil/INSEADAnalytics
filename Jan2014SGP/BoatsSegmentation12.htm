Our Approach

Clustering and Segmentation in 9 steps

  1. Confirm the data in metric

  2. Decide whether to scale or standardize the data

  3. Decide which variables to use for clustering

  4. Define similarity or dissimilarity measures between observations

  5. Visualize Individual Attributes and Pair-wise Distances between the Observations

  6. Select the clustering method to use and decide how many clusters to have

  7. Profile and interpret the clusters

  8. Assess the robustness of our clusters

Let's follow these steps.

2. Decide whether to scale or standardize the data

We do not necessarily need to scale the data because they are on the same magnitude. However, in this case, we standardized the data because of different measurements (e.g. length of boat).

ProjectData_scaled = apply(ProjectData, 2, function(r) {
    if (sd(r) != 0) 
        res = (r - mean(r))/sd(r) else res = 0 * r
    res
})

In the end, we found the 7 segments by using variables described above and came up with the most sensible story.

Step 4. Define similarity or dissimilarity measures between observations

In our case we explore two distance metrics: the commonly used Euclidean distance as well as a simple one we define manually.

The Euclidean distance between two observations (in our case, customers) is simply the square root of the average of the square difference between the attributes of the two observations (in our case, customers). Let's now define our own distance metric, as an example. Let's say that the management team of the company believes that two customers are similar if they do not differ in their ratings of the attitudinal questions by more than 2 points. We can manually assign a distance of 1 for every question for which two customers gave an answer that differs by more than 2 points, and 0 otherwise. It is easy to write this distance function in R:

My_Distance_function <- function(x, y) {
    sum(abs(x - y) > 2)
}

Step 6. Select the clustering method to use and decide how many clusters to have

At this stage, we need to decide what type of clustering we want to use as well as what number of clusters we would like to define. As the details and differences of the two types of clustering where outside of the scope of our course, we simply decided to stick with the default setting using the “hclust”-approach (as opposed to the “k-means”). Furthermore, we choose to stick to the pre-set Eucledian distance, as this provides a non-negative and continuous distance definition, which amplifies the effects of two widely separated data points (as it is the squared distance).

In order to determine the number of clusters which we define, we used trial and error until we obtained clusters which had a clear “identity”“ terms of questionnaire answers. Eventually, we determined the number of clusters which yielded the clearest pattern within the clusters (in terms of a cluster having a mean which deviates clearly the mean of the entire population), were seven.

Note that we can draw as many clusters are we choose (e.g. in this case we chose 7 clusters) around the branches of the Dendrogram.

We can also plot the "distances” traveled before we need to merge any of the lower and smaller in size clusters into larger ones - the heights of the tree branches that link the clusters as we traverse the tree from its leaves to its root. If we have n observations, this plot has n-1 numbers.

Step 7. Profile and interpret the clusters

After 6 rounds of iterations, we found 7 distinct customer segments. Below are the detailed profile description of the segments:

Segment 1: The Nouveau Boaters - Mostly men, not price sensitive, taking boat as a social activity, a new lifestyle experience

Segment 2: The Buffy Boaters - Mostly men, experienced boaters, knowledgeable and can perform repair, not price sensitive

Segment 3: The Wall Street financiers - Mostly men, brand and status are why they take on boats, just like with golf, country club, cars… - it's all about prestige and class

Segment 4: The Hippy Emma - These tend to be hippy younger inexperienced boaters, but if they take on boat, they prefer to repair things on their own, to be independent

Segment 5: Young Bill - The rising boaters, young, like to take on boating as a passion, but price sensitive

Segment 6: Cheap buy, preferring no frills

Segment 7: Disinterested customer segment
