Our Approach

Clustering and Segmentation in 9 steps

  1. Confirm the data in metric

  2. Decide whether to scale or standardize the data

  3. Decide which variables to use for clustering

  4. Define similarity or dissimilarity measures between observations

  5. Visualize Individual Attributes and Pair-wise Distances between the Observations

  6. Select the clustering method to use and decide how many clusters to have

  7. Profile and interpret the clusters

  8. Assess the robustness of our clusters

Let's follow these steps.

Step 4. Define similarity or dissimilarity measures between observations

In our case we explore two distance metrics: the commonly used Euclidean distance as well as a simple one we define manually.

The Euclidean distance between two observations (in our case, customers) is simply the square root of the average of the square difference between the attributes of the two observations (in our case, customers). Let's now define our own distance metric, as an example. Let's say that the management team of the company believes that two customers are similar if they do not differ in their ratings of the attitudinal questions by more than 2 points. We can manually assign a distance of 1 for every question for which two customers gave an answer that differs by more than 2 points, and 0 otherwise. It is easy to write this distance function in R:

My_Distance_function <- function(x, y) {
    sum(abs(x - y) > 2)

Step 6. Select the clustering method to use and decide how many clusters to have

The hierarchical clustering method was used.

Seven clusters were decided upon. An iterative analysis process revealed the following:

  1. The transition from six to seven clusters split an existing cluster into two new clusters. A qualitative analysis of the two clusters showed that they were both distinct and meaningful for marketing purposes.

  2. The transition from seven to eight clusters introduced a new cluster that was relatively indistinct from an existing cluster. Therefore, eight segments was where the creation of unmeaningful clusters began.

In this note we will use two widely used methods: the Kmeans Clustering Method, and the Hierarchical Clustering Method. Like all clustering methods, these two also require that we have decided how to measure the distance/similarity between our observations. Explaining how these methods work is beyond our scope. The only difference to highlight is that Kmeans requires the user to define how many segments to create, while Hierarchical Clustering does not.

Let's fist use the Hierarchial Clustering method, as we do not know for now how many segments there are in our data. Hierarchical clustering is a method that also helps us visualise how the data may be clustering together. It generates a plot called the Dendrogram which is often helpful for visualization - but should be used with care. Intepretation, Decisions and Conclusion

Based on the data and subsequent segment profiling, it would appear that CreeqBoat is positioned as a high-end brand in people's minds. Based on the segment profile analysis, it appears that those most likely to recommend or purchase CreeqBoat products are those that fall in the “Knowledgeable / Experienced Boaters” segment or in the “Power Status Seekers” segment. Those in the more price sensitive segments, such as young “Bang for Buck” respondents or “No Frills” respondents, were much less likely to highly consider CreeqBoat as a brand.

Those segments who were likely to recommend or purchase CreeqBoat products had certain key associations with the brand: trust, prestige, power, easy to maintain. These key associations seem to provide the justification to pay a higher price for CreeqBoat products. The brand seems to attract those who are looking for a brand they can be proud of owning. It is perhaps encouraging to note that the brand not only attracts older, autonomous and experienced boaters (connaisseurs) but also the younger, more power-hungry jet-setting consumer.

CreeqBoat must now decide if it wishes to remain positioned in the “high-end” brand segment or whether it wants to move more mass-market and cater to more price-sensitive consumers.

We would likely recommend that they capitalize on their brand equity in the “high-end” segment and concentrate marketing efforts on building up and maintaining the key brand associations (as explored above) that seem to attract the Experienced Boaters as well as the Power-Status Seekers. We would recommend not only clear marketing messages that reinforce this perception but also a continued commitment to quality in production (at the R&D and manufacturing level) in order to back up the brand equity.
