Business Decisions

In this assignment, we made a cluster analysis of the Boats dataset, aiming at identifying different segments within the dataset, using the five previously identified factors of importance. After several trial and error rounds, we identified five segments that show clear differences across several characteristics, such as the importance of boats in their lives, the usage of boats, the ownership of a boat, and demographic characteristics such as age and salary. This segment information can be used for targeted marketing and sales activities as well as for product development.

The Data

This dataset is composed of 29 questions related to customers value perception, usage and purchasing behavior related to boats. Our Approach

Clustering and Segmentation in 9 steps

  1. Confirm the data in metric

  2. Decide whether to scale or standardize the data

  3. Decide which variables to use for clustering

  4. Define similarity or dissimilarity measures between observations

  5. Visualize Individual Attributes and Pair-wise Distances between the Observations

  6. Select the clustering method to use and decide how many clusters to have

  7. Profile and interpret the clusters

  8. Assess the robustness of our clusters

Let's follow these steps.

Let's now define our own distance metric, as an example. Let's say that the management team of the company believes that two customers are similar if they do not differ in their ratings of the attitudinal questions by more than 2 points. We can manually assign a distance of 1 for every question for which two customers gave an answer that differs by more than 2 points, and 0 otherwise. It is easy to write this distance function in R:

My_Distance_function <- function(x, y) {
    sum(abs(x - y) > 2)
}

Step 6. Select the clustering method to use and decide how many clusters to have

We have tried both the Kmeans Clustering Method as well as the Hierarchical Clustering Method. Since the technical differences of these methods are out of scope for this exercise, we have considered both results and decided to use the clustering method that makes most sense to us. When using the Kmeans Clustering Method and 5 clusters, there are clear differences between the clusters, and the outcomes of the different variables are consistent (more on this later). When using fewer clusters, it becomes difficult to explain the cluster characteristics, as several apparently contradictory variables start to appear together. We believe to see this because when grouping the customers in only, for example, 3 groups, we are combining customers that do have very differentiating characteristics. On the other hand, when choosing many more segments, such as 9, it becomes very difficult to explain each segment and find the differentiating elements between them. Thus, we choose 5 clusters for this analysis.

In this note we will use two widely used methods: the Kmeans Clustering Method, and the Hierarchical Clustering Method. Like all clustering methods, these two also require that we have decided how to measure the distance/similarity between our observations. Explaining how these methods work is beyond our scope. The only difference to highlight is that Kmeans requires the user to define how many segments to create, while Hierarchical Clustering does not.

Let's fist use the Hierarchial Clustering method, as we do not know for now how many segments there are in our data. Hierarchical clustering is a method that also helps us visualise how the data may be clustering together. It generates a plot called the Dendrogram which is often helpful for visualization - but should be used with care. Step 7. Profile and interpret the clusters

As written above, we decided to use 5 segments (clusters) to separate the relevant different customer groups for the MyBoat case. Based on their features and main characteristics, we developed the 5 customer groups with the following names: 1 - Experienced boat nerds: They are the desperate boat users for a diverse set of activities. For them, boating is not about status symbol, it is their number one lifestyle, but they are still very price conscious in parallel. Also, they have a good brand awareness and knowledge about boats. 2- Poor wannabe guys: Really price sensitive group, many of whom are still just dreaming about having a boat and entering the cirlce of boat owners. They are definitely not boat experts at the moment, no strong connection to brands, rather want to have a simple, no-frill boat. 3 - Indifferent retirees: The oldest customer segment, for whom boating is one of the several activities which they do in their spare time. They are not necessarily experts in boating, engine performance is not important for them, however most of them are loyal to the brand of their existing boat. 4 - Conscious and adventurous yuppies: The youngest and most price sensitive group, who wants possibly everything but for a reasonable price. Their life is really concentrated around boating and water sports. Tend to follow trends and adapt easily, but not loyal. 5 - Posh, show-off guys: People who really see boating as a way to express and show their wealth situation to others. The least price sensitive customers who are ready to pay for the highest quality. They claim to have accurate knowledge about boats and tend to use their boat primarily for socializing with other people.

In our case, assuming we decided we use the 5 segments found using Kmeans as outlined above (similar profiling can be done with the results of hierarchical clustering or other segmentation methods), we can see how the responses to our survey differ across segments. Can we see differences between the segments? Do the segments differ in terms of their average household income and in terms of how often they visit the mall? What else can we say about these segments?

The 5 segments we identified strongly differs from each other on the following key characteristics:

  • Perceived importance of price vs brand
  • The role of the boat as a status symbol
  • How important is the role of boating in their free time and how passionate they are about boating
  • What is their loyalty to the brand of their current boat It seems that the average household income is not a strong differentiating factor among these segments. However, their actual income slightly correlates with their price sensitivity - segment 2 and 4 have the lowest average income and the highest price sensitivity. According to the current segmentation it seems that the motivation and purpose of boating separate more clearly these 5 segments than their income level. Only Question 1 tells us anything about the shopping habits of the different segments. (How many dealers do they visit when buying a boat) According to the data in our segmentation, the 5 clusters do not significantly differ among this dimension. Their average answer for this question is close to the total populations average in each 5 cases, therefore we can not conclude that the customers in the 5 segments would have strongly differenciated boat shopping habits.

Intepretation, Decisions and Conclusion

We believe that CreeqBoat can actually use this segmantation to design and execute better its business and product strategy. In the earlier explanations, we already identified those critical dimensions which have the strongest differentiating power across the segments and which can be the best use for the company. Based on this, CreeqBoat should either choose a niche strategy and focus on selected 1-2 segments or adjust its product portfolio to the 5 segments with at least 3 different categories: 1. simple basic boats, 2. strong performance boats, 3. high quality luxury boats. Obviously, price categories also should be clearly differentiated, so that the company can meet the different levels of willingness to pay. However, one should also realize, that the development of these 5 categories from the data provided was arbitrary by the nature of diverse data analysis. Obviously, we only had a limited time to work with these data and to develop our segments plus conclusions. As discussed during the class, one could run these analysis forever and still not feel fully convinced about the end results. Also, the potential bias of human information processing came into picture most of the time. When we were trying to develop and describe out selected segments, we unintentionally looked back all the time for our real life experiences, pictured potential boat users and brought in some additional inputs which were not necessary presented in the data. But even beside this, we can conclude that CreeqBoat is definitely provided with more accurate analysis after this description of the 5 segments, than if they only had analyzie the total population averages and deviations for certain questions. It is absolutely critical to find the trade-off between the complexity and deepness of analysis and the additional insights gained from it.
