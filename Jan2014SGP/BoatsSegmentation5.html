Our Approach

Clustering and Segmentation in 9 steps

  1. Confirm the data in metric

  2. Decide whether to scale or standardize the data

  3. Decide which variables to use for clustering

  4. Define similarity or dissimilarity measures between observations

  5. Visualize Individual Attributes and Pair-wise Distances between the Observations

  6. Select the clustering method to use and decide how many clusters to have

  7. Profile and interpret the clusters

  8. Assess the robustness of our clusters

Let's follow these steps.

Step 4. Define similarity or dissimilarity measures between observations

In our case we explore two distance metrics: the commonly used Euclidean distance as well as a simple one we define manually.

The Euclidean distance between two observations (in our case, customers) is simply the square root of the average of the square difference between the attributes of the two observations (in our case, customers). Let's now define our own distance metric, as an example. Let's say that the management team of the company believes that two customers are similar if they do not differ in their ratings of the attitudinal questions by more than 2 points. We can manually assign a distance of 1 for every question for which two customers gave an answer that differs by more than 2 points, and 0 otherwise. It is easy to write this distance function in R:

My_Distance_function <- function(x, y) {
    sum(abs(x - y) > 2)
}

Step 6. Select the clustering method to use and decide how many clusters to have

For our final clustering we have selected the Kmeans Clustering Method, however both have been analyzed below.

In this note we will use two widely used methods: the Kmeans Clustering Method, and the Hierarchical Clustering Method. Like all clustering methods, these two also require that we have decided how to measure the distance/similarity between our observations. Explaining how these methods work is beyond our scope. The only difference to highlight is that Kmeans requires the user to define how many segments to create, while Hierarchical Clustering does not.

Let's fist use the Hierarchial Clustering method, as we do not know for now how many segments there are in our data. Hierarchical clustering is a method that also helps us visualise how the data may be clustering together. It generates a plot called the Dendrogram which is often helpful for visualization - but should be used with care. Note that we can draw as many clusters are we choose (e.g. in this case we chose 5 clusters) around the branches of the Dendrogram.

We can also plot the “distances” traveled before we need to merge any of the lower and smaller in size clusters into larger ones - the heights of the tree branches that link the clusters as we traverse the tree from its leaves to its root. If we have n observations, this plot has n-1 numbers.

Bearing in mind certain slightly counterintuitive data points across customer segments (e.g. a higher score for employment actually signifies that unemployment is higher on average in that customer segment), we identified 5 principal customer segments (please bear in mind that when this webpage is reloaded, the results seem to vary very slightly, leading to minor changes in the data, e.g. segment columns being interchanged, etc.):

Segment 1: "Boating Soccer Moms": this segment comprises more females than others; they strongly prefer low maintenance/low DIY, want to use the boat with groups of people, can't recommend their boat to others yet would want to buy the same model again for themselves as it presumably fits their purposes

Segment 2: "Dreamers" segment: this segment consists of younger, well employed people, but with relatively low incomes. They dream of high quality boats (perhaps expecting their incomes to go up to allow them such purchases), are willing to perform repairs themselves and target an interesting mix of high quality yet low prices. Perhaps in anticipation of an increase in their income, while they recommend their boat to friends, they believe they will purchase a different boat in the future

Segment 3: "Do it yourself": this segment contains DIY an older demographic with a secondary interest in boating. They don't view their boat as a status symbol (but presumably solely as a means of enjoyment) and are willing to undertake repairs and maintenance on their own

Segment 4: "Super premium": this segment is made up of those who want the latest and greatest boats, the best brands and who care little about cost. Looking into age and income, we could assume that this segment is full of younger, well-employed people who have already attained above average income. In an interesting twist, though they recommend their boat to others, they would prefer another boat in the future (perhaps already considering an upgrade).

Segment 5: "No frills": this segment contains those who are looking for a boat but want to spend the least money possible and don't consider boating their primary passion in life. In addition, this demographic is the least employed and the eldest, would not at all recommend their current boat to other users, but believe that their current boat is the right option for themselves in the future

Intepretation, Decisions and Conclusion

So in the end, using 13 factors and clustering the data into 5 segments, we reach the customer segments described above. These 5 segments can be easily supplied with:

i) a premium product line

ii) a low-maintenance boat line

iii) a no frills boat line

These product categories directly address the Super Premium, Soccer Moms, and No Frills/DIY segments. Presumably the Dreamers can either stretch to purchase a Premium boat, or retain money and buy a Soccer Mom or No Frills boat. As for the DIY segment, they can find whatever between the Soccer Moms and No Frills lines is easiest to tinker around with and most modular.

Therefore, thanks to this analysis we believe we can build up our product lines in such a way as to best fulfill customer wants and therefore boost our sales to the maximum thanks to our deep customer understanding. This should give our company a significant advantage in winning over new customers and retaining old ones.
