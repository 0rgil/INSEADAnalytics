Business Decisions

In response to a slow down in the boating market, CreeqBoat is looking into different growth opportunities, mainly in the North America region. In order to effectively assess their options and potential, CreeqBoat needs to better understand their customers and their purchasing preference and behaviors. They will need to analzye which different segments of customers are in the market, what each specific segment wants and needs in a boat and what CreeqBoat could or should offer them.

In order to arrive at such a business strategy, CreeqBoat has approached a market research firm to gather data in the US market on boating and boating preferences.

The Data

The data used as a basis for the segmentation were the results of a survey on boating behavior, preference and attitudes. Here, 82 questions were asked and scored by almost 3,000 boat owners or people intending to buy a boat. The questions included 29 behavioral questions supplemented by questions about demographics and situational preference. The behavioral questions were scored on a 5-point scale, others on a binary metric scale (e.g. gender) making the results of the survey metric data. As 29 different attitudes and behaviors were too complex and varied to use to determine different clusters of respondents in the data, we grouped the data according to six different factors in the step prior to this exercise, whilst trying to maintain as much of the variance within the data as possible. Based on these factors, which tell us something about the different motivations for buying boats, we will now cluster our respondents into different groups of persona - differentiated by their socioeconomic status, demographic characteriscs and boating preferences and attitudes that will help us identify how to best tailor our marketing strategy to these consumers.

Our Approach

Clustering and Segmentation in 9 steps

  1. Confirm the data in metric

  2. Decide whether to scale or standardize the data

  3. Decide which variables to use for clustering

  4. Define similarity or dissimilarity measures between observations

  5. Visualize Individual Attributes and Pair-wise Distances between the Observations

  6. Select the clustering method to use and decide how many clusters to have

  7. Profile and interpret the clusters

  8. Assess the robustness of our clusters

Let's follow these steps.

Step 3. Decide which variables to use for clustering

The factor analysis revealed a number of distinct motivations for the survey respondents, which we could use to see whether specific segments within the respondents exist. Based on the analysis specific questions or clusters of questions revealed specific attitudes or behaviors within the population. Specifically, questions 1.21, 1.27, 1.09, 1.04, 1.02 and 1.11 were shown to significantly stand out in determining the variance between reponses in the survey data.

These questions were indicated as input for the cluster analysis, where the program searches for clusters of respondents that answer similarly to the indicated questions. In other words, the program looks which clusters of respondents have the same behavior or attitude, based on the six distinct behavioral attitudes we identified in the previous analysis.

As the analysis of “big” or diverse data is an iterative process, we changed the amount of clusters varying from 4 to 8, ultimately settling on 5 clusters based on our interpretation and the stability of the clusters with varying methods of analysis.

Step 4. Define similarity or dissimilarity measures between observations

In our case we explore two distance metrics: the commonly used Euclidean distance as well as a simple one we define manually.

The Euclidean distance between two observations (in our case, customers) is simply the square root of the average of the square difference between the attributes of the two observations (in our case, customers). Let's now define our own distance metric, as an example. Let's say that the management team of the company believes that two customers are similar if they do not differ in their ratings of the attitudinal questions by more than 2 points. We can manually assign a distance of 1 for every question for which two customers gave an answer that differs by more than 2 points, and 0 otherwise. It is easy to write this distance function in R:

My_Distance_function <- function(x, y) {
    sum(abs(x - y) > 2)
}

Step 6. Select the clustering method to use and decide how many clusters to have

As the analysis of such data sets is an iterative process, we experimented with different cluster numbers (four to eight to be exact) in combination with the six factors we determined to represent distinct motivational drivers. In our opinion, the most clearly separable and defined target consumer profiles were derived when running five clusters with six factors.

In order to determine whether our clusters were stable, we then re-ran six factors and five clusters under Kmeans and the hierarchical clustering method. Another verification or tool for justification of a certain number of clusters is the dendogram, which visually shows a plot of distances between different (groups of) respondents. In the case that we choose 5 clusters for the survey responses, the dendogram shows 5 similarly large and distinct groups of respondents, each group in split up in approximately a similar distance from each other and it seems each group is approximately equally large and spread out. When trying to apply different number of clusters to this data, the dendogram showed differently sized clusters or cut off the clusters at different distances. The clusters remained stable, hence we finalized our choice of factors and clusters.

In this note we will use two widely used methods: the Kmeans Clustering Method, and the Hierarchical Clustering Method. Like all clustering methods, these two also require that we have decided how to measure the distance/similarity between our observations. Explaining how these methods work is beyond our scope. The only difference to highlight is that Kmeans requires the user to define how many segments to create, while Hierarchical Clustering does not.

Let's fist use the Hierarchial Clustering method, as we do not know for now how many segments there are in our data. Hierarchical clustering is a method that also helps us visualise how the data may be clustering together. It generates a plot called the Dendrogram which is often helpful for visualization - but should be used with care. Step 7. Profile and interpret the clusters

Cluster 1 : [Socialite Sailor (Wo)Man] Our first cluster is dominated by women. This cluster is interested in boating as a social activity but isn't particularly knowledgeable about boats. While they do view boats as a status symbol and it is the number 1 activity in their spare time, the fact that their knowledge on boats is low indicates that this group are not fanatic boaters. Possible consumers could be socialite wives of fanatic boaters who recognize the importance of sailing as a social event or their young, professional daughters. Potentially, this also includes young professionals who grew up in a boating household and now practice it as member of a club on occasion.

Cluster 2: [The Bulls of Sail Street] This cluster is male dominated. Its most pertinent characteristic are the importance of power and status that comes with boating. This group shows little price sensitivity and wants its boats to come with all the frills possible. While boating is a sport to this group and they are very knowledgeable about the sport itself as well as the equipment, boating is first and foremost a manifestation of their success.

Cluster 3: [Future Yacht Leaders of America]: This cluster is characterized by a predominantly younger age demographic. Like their (potentially Cluster 2) fathers, this generation values boating as both a sport as well as a symbol of success, achievement ad power. However, at this stage of their life they are price sensitive - hence leading to them not being able to own their equipment. However, they are differentiated from the no frills buyer in the sense that they can appreciate the quality of a brand name boat and would consider spending money on it, could they afford it.

Cluster 4: [Fresh off the Boat] This cluster of customers is characterized by utter and complete novices. This profile group doesn't own any of their own boating equipment nor are they particularly knowledgeable about boating (yet). However, it should be noted that this could potentially stem from newness alone and as this group is characterized by high employment rate, it is a cluster worth monitoring/targeting.

Cluster 5: [Sea Rex] This cluster is demographically dominated by older men who don't care about bells and whistles on their boats. They are knowledgeable about boats, consider it an important past time, are familiar with techniques. While they do appreciate good craftsmenship, they do not care about the status a boat brings or any of the additional frills buying a certain brand brings.

Intepretation, Decisions and Conclusion

Based on the factor analysis we derived that there are six distinct motivations with which different individuals can be split up and clustered. Using these motivational drivers, we foudn five different segments in the survey respondents, that are representative of the boating market. These segments are the following: Socialite Sailor (Wo)men, The Bulls of Sail Street, Future Yacht Leaders of America, Fresh off the Boat and Sea Rex. Creating a solid understanding of these groups will help CreeqBoat to develop customized and targeted offerings to specific groups and individuals to realize their growth and potential in the US market
