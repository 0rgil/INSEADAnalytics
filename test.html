Business Decisions

The purpose of this exercise is to identify the main factors which characterize boat consumers and influences their boat purchasing decisions, and to use those factors as a basis for creating a market segmentation. The segmentation, will allow CreeqBoat to focus its marketing on the most promising customers, and to identify new growth opportunities.

The Data

A market research firm has compiled data about the US boating market from almost 3,000 interviews. The data describes attitudes towards boating, demographics, information about boats, use of boats, pricing and brand equity.

Our Approach

Factor Analysis in 6 steps

  1. Confirm the data in metric

  2. Decide whether to scale or standardize the data

  3. Check the correlation matrix to see if Factor Analysis makes sense

  4. Develop a scree plot and decide on the number of factors to be derived

  5. Interpret the factors (consider factor rotations - technical but useful)

  6. Save factor scores for subsequent analyses

Step 2: Decide whether to scale or standardize the data

We have decided not to scale the data, as they are all already on a Likert scale from 1-5, and so comparable. Standardization would dampen differences between questions that provoked more or less extreme responses – we want to preserve the “overweighting” effect of strong opinions, and so will not standardise.

ProjectDatafactor_scaled = apply(ProjectDataFactor, 2, function(r) {
    if (sd(r) != 0) 
        res = (r - mean(r))/sd(r) else res = 0 * r
    res
})

Step 4. Develop a scree plot and decide on the number of factors to be derived

Initially, we decided to use an eigenvalue criterion to cut the data, and settled on 5 factors. We tried 9, and observed that several of these contained only single questions; these did not seem more relevant to use as standalone criteria than other questions which had been bundled together. So we went with five factors.

When running the cluster analysis, we realised that five factors did not give us sufficient granularity to effectively define segments. So we re-ran the factor analysis to ensure that 65% of the original variance was retained. This gave us 10 principal components.




Eigenvalue analysis suggests that 5 factors should be enough (see scree plot above). However, as mentioned earlier, this did not help us with the clustering, as around half the information is lost by generating that number of factors.

We finally settled on a criterion of including enough factors to explain 65% of the variance.

Intepretation, Decisions and Conclusion

The 10 factors identified above can be thought of as the dimensions along which groups of customers' purchasing behaviour can be influenced. Cluster analysis will help us identify observable groups of customers to whom that influence can be targeted; relating those clusters back to these dimensions will help us identify which marketing attributes will drive purchasing behaviour among whom. That will allow us to optimise the marketing mix.

We could equally have settled on 8, 10 or 12 factors. With more insight into the boat market, we would likely have been able to make a better judgement call. However, given our current knowledge, the fact that these 10 factors can be distinguished from each other and allow us to develop a reasonable segmentation, suggests that this is “good enough” for the purposes of the exercise.

Of course, for an actual company whose profits depend on getting this right, the level of rigour applied should be higher.
