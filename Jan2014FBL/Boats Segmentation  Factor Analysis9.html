Factor Analysis for Dimensionality Reduction for CreeqBoat

The “Business Decision”

The team at Creeqboat has to develop a growth strategy for the North American market. They needed to understand the current and potential customers in the market to enable them to build more targeted boats for their most important customer segments there.

The Approach

Factor analysis is used to find a few “hidden” derived attributes in data by combining together groups of the original raw attributes in such a way that the least information in the original data is lost - in a statistical sense.

We can reduce the data to a more understandable form so that our decision making process can be made potentially simpler, more actionable, and easier to interpret and justify - without losing much information in the original data. It is much easier to make tradeoffs between few attributes than for 29 attributes.

A Process for Dimensionality Reduction

Factor Analysis in 6 steps

We use the 6 step factor analysis process:

  1. Confirm the data in metric

  2. Decide whether to scale or standardize the data

  3. Check the correlation matrix to see if Factor Analysis makes sense

  4. Develop a scree plot and decide on the number of factors to be derived

  5. Interpret the factors and consider factor rotations (technical but useful)

  6. Save factor scores for subsequent analyses

Step 1: Confirm the data are metric

Steps 1-3 are about specific descriptive characteristics of the data. In particular, the methods we consider in this note require that the data are metric (step 1): this means not only that all data are numbers, but also that the numbers have an actual numerical meaning, that is 1 is less than 2 which is less than 3 etc.

Step 4. Develop a scree plot and decide on the number of factors to be derived

In order to decide how many factors to derive, we have done multiple iterations based on the three criteria (eigenvalue > 1, cumulative percentage of variance explained, and the elbow of the scree plot). We achieve the most optimum selection of factors that capture most of the explained variance by using our current selection criterion, namely manual and we decided to use 8 components only.







5. Interpret the factors and consider factor rotations

For our data we “rotated” (mathematically) the original factors to generate new ones wich are easier to interpret while capturing the same information from the raw data as the initial factors found. For our data, the 8 selected factors look as follows after the varimax rotation:



To better visualize and interpret the factors “supressed” loadings with small values, e.g. with absolute values smaller than 0.5. In this case our factors look as follows after suppressing the small numbers:



After rotation each factor combines (we say “loads on”) only a few of the original raw attributes, making interpretation easier. For example, if we only select the factors with eigenvalue more than 1, in this case we would select 5 factors.

We interpreted the selected factors using the three Factor Technical Quality Criteria:

  1. For each factor (column) only a few loadings are large (in absolute value)

  2. For each raw attribute (row) only a few loadings are large (in absolute value)

  3. Any pair of factors (columns) should have different “patterns” of loading

Based on our interpretation, we observe the following patterns in the factors:

1. Component 1: Product Indulgence
The attributes assigned to component 1 describe the passion associated with boating to a user. They reflect on the emotional satisfaction derived by indulging in boating activities.

2. Component 2: Product Experience
This captures the attributes which describe the rewarding experiences that buyers get from using the boat i.e. adventure, socializing, taking time off.

3. Component 3: Status symbol
The attributes assigned to this component are derived from the desire to project a status symbol by having a boat which is superior. This factor captures the external signalling aspiration when one buys a boat.

4. Component 4: Value for money
This factor combines the attributes which define the worth of the boat for the money that the customers are willing to pay for it.

5. Component 5: Brand Value
This factor is associated with the attributes related to the trust the customers place in the brand and the quality associated with the brand.

6. Component 6: Technical appeal
This component groups the attributes that satiates the technical curiosity of the customers.

7. Component 7: Purchase involvement
This refers to the attribute of how involved the customer is while buying a boat.

8. Component 8: Functional value
This factor captures the utilitarian appeal of the boat versus the style aspect to the customer.

Step 6. Save factor scores for subsequent analyses

Once we decided the factors to use (for now), we typically replace the original data with a new dataset where each observation (row) is now described not using the original raw attributes but using instead the selected factors/derived attributes. Afterall this was the goal of this analysis.

The way to represent our observations using the found derived attributes (factors/components) is to estimate for each observation (row) how it “scores” for each of the selected factor. These numbers are called factor scores. Effectively they are the “scores” the observation would take on the factor had we measured that factor directly instead of measuring the original raw attributes.
