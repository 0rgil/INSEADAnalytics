Business Decisions

Given the data provided and based on the analyses, our team decided to group the participants under 4 major segments: The Casual Boaters, Boaters from the Heart, Functional Boaters, and Status Seekers

The Data

The data is the result of an 81 question survey that was filled out by 2813 boat users which includes questions such as the reasons for purchasing a boat, desired price range, and potential uses for the boat. Analyzing this data using big data analytics approach have provided us with the desired outcome of successfully segmenting the boat users.

Our Approach

Clustering and Segmentation in 9 steps

  1. Confirm the data in metric

  2. Decide whether to scale or standardize the data

  3. Decide which variables to use for clustering

  4. Define similarity or dissimilarity measures between observations

  5. Visualize Individual Attributes and Pair-wise Distances between the Observations

  6. Select the clustering method to use and decide how many clusters to have

  7. Profile and interpret the clusters

  8. Assess the robustness of our clusters

Let's follow these steps.

Step 4. Define similarity or dissimilarity measures between observations

In our case we explore two distance metrics: the commonly used Euclidean distance as well as a simple one we define manually.

The Euclidean distance between two observations (in our case, customers) is simply the square root of the average of the square difference between the attributes of the two observations (in our case, customers). Let's now define our own distance metric, as an example. Let's say that the management team of the company believes that two customers are similar if they do not differ in their ratings of the attitudinal questions by more than 2 points. We can manually assign a distance of 1 for every question for which two customers gave an answer that differs by more than 2 points, and 0 otherwise. It is easy to write this distance function in R:

My_Distance_function <- function(x, y) {
    sum(abs(x - y) > 2)
}

Step 6. Select the clustering method to use and decide how many clusters to have

Using these 13 factors, we tested segmenting the population into groups of 3 , 4, 5, and 6 to see how the plots would appear and picked the number of clusters that gave the cleanest snake plot. According to the snake plot, 4 segments gave the cleanest breakout of segment population over all the profiling attributes. Hence we decided to go with this.

In this note we will use two widely used methods: the Kmeans Clustering Method, and the Hierarchical Clustering Method. Like all clustering methods, these two also require that we have decided how to measure the distance/similarity between our observations. Explaining how these methods work is beyond our scope. The only difference to highlight is that Kmeans requires the user to define how many segments to create, while Hierarchical Clustering does not.

Let's fist use the Hierarchial Clustering method, as we do not know for now how many segments there are in our data. Hierarchical clustering is a method that also helps us visualise how the data may be clustering together. It generates a plot called the Dendrogram which is often helpful for visualization - but should be used with care. For example, in this case the dendrogram, using the euclidean distance metric from the earlier steps and the ward hierarchical clustering option (see below as well as help(hclust) in R for more information), is as follows:

Note that we can draw as many clusters are we choose (e.g. in this case we chose 4 clusters) around the branches of the Dendrogram.

We can also plot the “distances” traveled before we need to merge any of the lower and smaller in size clusters into larger ones - the heights of the tree branches that link the clusters as we traverse the tree from its leaves to its root. If we have n observations, this plot has n-1 numbers.

Intepretation, Decisions and Conclusion

Using the resulting data, we came up with the following segmentation:

Segment 1: These are the “Casual Boaters”. Most likely, they look at boating as a means to an end, a way to spend time with family and friends. They do not know much about boats and if their boat breaks down they will call the expert. They need to be told what to buy for their no-nonsense yet functional needs.

Segment 2: These are the “Boaters from the Heart”. They are expert boaters and are passionate about boating. They would gladly pay a premium for features but it must be justified by performance.

Segment 3: These are the “Functional Boaters”. They like boating but not as much as the people in segment 2. Boating is one of the important things for them; however, they are most interested in functionality vs status/reputation. They don't want any frills and will buy a basic model.

Segment 4: These are the “Status Seekers”. They are young people who are in love with the idea of boating because it seems romantic. Even though they are young and least likely to buy boats, to them the idea of boating conveys status and lifestyle. They would like to eventually purchase the best boats that the market has to offer and would like to spend money on it, but if it actually comes to purchasing they would be the most price conscious.
