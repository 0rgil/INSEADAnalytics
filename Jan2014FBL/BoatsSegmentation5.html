Business Decisions

The team at Creeqboat has to develop a growth strategy for the North American boating market. The main business decision CreeqBoat is facing is the segmentation of their clients to tailor products that best cater to the needs of the different segments, allowing CreeqBoat to skim the maximum profit from each segment.

The Data

The foundation of the analysis to find the best split into target segments is the information about the boating market Creeqboat gathered through 3000 interviews, which consisted of 81 questionnaires about customers' attitudes/needs/motivation toward boating, purchase process and behaviour, demographics, and brand equity.

Our Approach

Clustering and Segmentation in 9 steps

  1. Confirm the data in metric

  2. Decide whether to scale or standardize the data

  3. Decide which variables to use for clustering

  4. Define similarity or dissimilarity measures between observations

  5. Visualize Individual Attributes and Pair-wise Distances between the Observations

  6. Select the clustering method to use and decide how many clusters to have

  7. Profile and interpret the clusters

  8. Assess the robustness of our clusters

Let's follow these steps.

Step 4. Define similarity or dissimilarity measures between observations

In our case we explore two distance metrics: the commonly used Euclidean distance as well as a simple one we define manually.

The Euclidean distance between two observations (in our case, customers) is simply the square root of the average of the square difference between the attributes of the two observations (in our case, customers). Let's now define our own distance metric, as an example. Let's say that the management team of the company believes that two customers are similar if they do not differ in their ratings of the attitudinal questions by more than 2 points. We can manually assign a distance of 1 for every question for which two customers gave an answer that differs by more than 2 points, and 0 otherwise. It is easy to write this distance function in R:

My_Distance_function <- function(x, y) {
    sum(abs(x - y) > 2)
}

Step 6. Select the clustering method to use and decide how many clusters to have

Regarding the clustering and segmentation methods, we followed the guidance in BDA class.

In this note we will use two widely used methods: the Kmeans Clustering Method, and the Hierarchical Clustering Method. Like all clustering methods, these two also require that we have decided how to measure the distance/similarity between our observations. Explaining how these methods work is beyond our scope. The only difference to highlight is that Kmeans requires the user to define how many segments to create, while Hierarchical Clustering does not.

Let's fist use the Hierarchial Clustering method, as we do not know for now how many segments there are in our data. Hierarchical clustering is a method that also helps us visualise how the data may be clustering together. It generates a plot called the Dendrogram which is often helpful for visualization - but should be used with care. Note that we can draw as many clusters are we choose (e.g. in this case we chose 5 clusters) around the branches of the Dendrogram.

We can also plot the “distances” traveled before we need to merge any of the lower and smaller in size clusters into larger ones - the heights of the tree branches that link the clusters as we traverse the tree from its leaves to its root. If we have n observations, this plot has n-1 numbers.

Step 7. Profile and interpret the clusters

For profiling, in order to choose the attributes and demographic factors, that best separate the segments in the most distinct manner, we did a number of iterations.

In our case, assuming we decided we use the 5 segments found using Kmeans as outlined above (similar profiling can be done with the results of hierarchical clustering or other segmentation methods), we can see how the responses to our survey differ across segments. The average values of our data for the total population as well as within each customer segment are:

We can also “visualize” the segments using snake plots for each cluster. For example, we can plot the means of the profiling variables for each of our clusters to better visualize differences between segments. For better visualization we plot the standardized profiling variables.

We first started with using all possible attributes (2 till 82) and analysed the data. We found that there was not much difference across segments on almost 40% of the attributes i.e. the values were same as population average. Then we did a second iteration wherein we removed these non-differentiating attributes. Now we found that there were still some attributes on which two or more segments showed similar inclination viz. values for ratio for the average of the cluster to the average of the population were very close. This was not helpful in segregating the segments and hence we removed such attributes as well. However, we did not disregard demographic attributes such as gender, household income, age and descriptors like owning a boat in each of these iterations, so we dug deeper into those fields in a third iteration, leading us to deeper insight. Finally in the third iteration, we achieved a satisfactory mix of attributes which were helpful in sharply differentiating the segments. Thus we arrived at 37 attributes for profiling.

We can also compare the averages of the profiling variables of each segment relative to the average of the variables across the whole population. This can also help us better understand whether there are indeed clusters in our data (e.g. if all segments are much like the overall population, there may be no segments). Interpretation, Decisions and Conclusion

Based on the variance observed in the selected attributes, we have profiled the 5 segments as described below. Additionally we have proposed a strategy to target each of these segments. The choice of focus on a particular segment will be based on Creeqboat's objectives.

Segment 1 - Show offs
Interpretation: This segment is status conscious, willing to pay premium price, more concerned about style and less about functionality. This segment is male dominated and less engaged in technical aspects and mostly use boats to show off and project their status.
Strategy: These customers can be targeted by offering them a premium and stylish product with lots of frills and high brand equity. Additionally there can be upselling on other premium products related to boating.
Segment 2 - Boating enthusiasts
Interpretation: These customers are brand and status conscious. The technical aspects of boating appeal to them. They spend a lot of time boating and are very passionate about it, although the brand and “show-off factor” is not that much important as with the segment 1.
Strategy: Target this segment with highly functional and stylish products. This segment should be offered a range in boat models and accessories. They can also be offered long term services and accessories for subsequent future sales. Marketing and advertisement should focus on providing them a boating experience at the time of buying.
Segment 3 - Casual boaters
Interpretation: These are middle aged people who are not concerned with the status symbol and not very passionate about boating. These might include buyers who perhaps represent tour companies/other functional users and are concerned more with price and the functional aspects of the boat.
Strategy: Basic low cost model can be offered to this segment without accessories as they want a low frills product.
Segment 4 - Non-boaters
Interpretation: This segment is not enthusiastic about boating. They are not concerned with functionality, technical specs and brand. They do not have much experience in boating.
Strategy: They might represent an untapped market so the strategy should be focused on introducing them to the boating experience and convert them into future customers through basic products.
Segment 5 - Young status seekers
Interpretation: This is a relatively young segment that perceive boats as a status symbol but are price conscious at the same time. They are passionate and knowledgeable about boating.
Strategy: Offer them a product which is more stylish to project status but less functional in order to allow a reduction in price.
