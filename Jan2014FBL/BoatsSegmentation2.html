Business Decisions

How many market segments (clusters) are there? Why?

We decided on 5 market segments or clusters for for 3 reasons:

(1) Based on the denondogram and the diagnostic histogram, these are the clusters that seem the most widely dispersed. This is important because we are looking for high factor loadings in each cluster, leading to different approaches/strategies for each cluster.

(2) The sample size for each of the cluster is high and will likely be significant for both cross-tab and regression. Moreover, when discussing findings/insights with senior management, we can be confident that we are recommending solutions based on a relatively large sample size (i.e. not a sample of 10-20, which could be the case if we decide to split the data into more clusters).

(3)We ran analysis for 3, 4, 5, 6 clusters with 17, 15, 11, 9, and 6 factors each time. The combination of 6 factors and 5 clusters seemed to have yielded the most significant factor loadings and cluster variation without sacrificing sample size.

How would you describe the segments?

Segment 1: he is an expert user, and boating is a favorite spare time activity. He sees the boat as a status symbol, but he is very price sensitive.

Segment 2: Similar to segment 1, the customer cares about boating and sees the boat as a status symbol. The key difference is however, he is less price sensitive and finds the brand very important. Segment 2 has also higher income than segment 1, which makes sense given the price sensitivity of the two groups.

Segment 3: He is not very interested in his boat. In addition, his key attribute is that he do not want to do anything himself.

Segment 4: Similar characteristics to segment three. He is less interested in the brand, and the is highly price sensitive. He is however willing to do work on the boat by himself. This is the segment with the lowest average income.

Segment 5: The customer do not at all see the boat as a status symbol. He has average income and is not too concerned about shopping for the lowest price. Similarly to segment three and four boating is not is his favorite spare time activity.

How would the segments inform the strategy of CreeqBoat?

For segment 1 we want to create a boat of high quality with a lot of value for money. Focusing on practical solutions for frequent users more than fancy luxury details. This is in our view the Volkswagen segment.

For segment 2 we want to create a more luxurious offering, with a strong brand name and position. The boat has to be of high quality and fit with the demands of frequent usage. This is the Mercedes segment.

Segment 3 looks very challenging to serve as the user is both extremely price sensitive and he does not want to do anything on his own. One option could be to offer a cheap weekly rental service, covering all maintenance, cleaning etc. This is our National Car Rental segment.

Segment 4 requires a boat that has a lot of value, but brand is of little importance and he uses the boat a lot less than the customer in segment 1. We will thus serve this customer group by offering a affordable boat with few features. This is our Skoda customer.

Segment 5 don???t seem to be too interested in boating, and it might be very hard to sell a new boat to this customer. He might interested in a second hand boat service as he is willing to do some work himself.

In short we recommend focusing on serving customers in segment 1, 2 and 4.

Our Approach

Clustering and Segmentation in 9 steps

  1. Confirm the data in metric

  2. Decide whether to scale or standardize the data

  3. Decide which variables to use for clustering

  4. Define similarity or dissimilarity measures between observations

  5. Visualize Individual Attributes and Pair-wise Distances between the Observations

  6. Select the clustering method to use and decide how many clusters to have

  7. Profile and interpret the clusters

  8. Assess the robustness of our clusters

Let's follow these steps.

Step 3. Decide which variables to use for clustering

We base our choice of variables for the clustering on our factor analysis. Specifically, we chose one question - the one with the highest load - from each factor. In addition, we have added “Household Income” as a variable do be able to distinguish the clusters on their purchasing power.

Please recall that we chose 6 factors that together explain 55.84% of the variance in the data. By sorting each factor after load size we found the questions in the survey that relate to the different factors. Our factors suggested the following customer segments:

Factor 1: lifestyle-adventure driven customer that responds to the emotional aspects of owning a boat

Factor 2: expert user that focus on quality and technical features of the boat

Factor 3: conspicuous show-off consumer that mostly focus on the extent to which a particular type and brand of boat can increase his/her social status

Factor 4: brand-quality driven customer whose focus is on the cost/benefit of the functionality of the boat

Factor 5: rational-value conscious customer who is similar to segment 4 but that is less impressed by a brand but more focused on functionality

Factor 6: DIY customer who responds to the level of maintenance of the boat and the ease of repairability

Step 4. Define similarity or dissimilarity measures between observations

In our case we explore two distance metrics: the commonly used Euclidean distance as well as a simple one we define manually. Finally, we chose the Euclidean distance.

The Euclidean distance between two observations (in our case, customers) is simply the square root of the average of the square difference between the attributes of the two observations (in our case, customers). For example, the distance of the first customer in our data from customers 2-5 (summarized above), using their responses to the 6 attitudinal questions is:

Let's now define our own distance metric, as an example. Let's say that the management team of the company believes that two customers are similar if they do not differ in their ratings of the attitudinal questions by more than 2 points. We can manually assign a distance of 1 for every question for which two customers gave an answer that differs by more than 2 points, and 0 otherwise. It is easy to write this distance function in R:

My_Distance_function <- function(x, y) {
    sum(abs(x - y) > 2)
}

Step 6. Select the clustering method to use and decide how many clusters to have

We experiemented with different number of clusters and observed that the number of clusters affected our ability to distinguish between them. We ended up choosing 5 clusters as this showed five clearly different segments that we believe are relevant and actionable for the management.

Metode:

In this note we will use two widely used methods: the Kmeans Clustering Method, and the Hierarchical Clustering Method. Like all clustering methods, these two also require that we have decided how to measure the distance/similarity between our observations. Explaining how these methods work is beyond our scope. The only difference to highlight is that Kmeans requires the user to define how many segments to create, while Hierarchical Clustering does not.

Let's fist use the Hierarchial Clustering method, as we do not know for now how many segments there are in our data. Hierarchical clustering is a method that also helps us visualise how the data may be clustering together. It generates a plot called the Dendrogram which is often helpful for visualization - but should be used with care. For example, in this case the dendrogram, using the euclidean distance metric from the earlier steps and the ward hierarchical clustering option (see below as well as help(hclust) in R for more information), is as follows:

Note that we can draw as many clusters are we choose (e.g. in this case we chose 9 clusters) around the branches of the Dendrogram.

We can also plot the “distances” traveled before we need to merge any of the lower and smaller in size clusters into larger ones - the heights of the tree branches that link the clusters as we traverse the tree from its leaves to its root. If we have n observations, this plot has n-1 numbers.
