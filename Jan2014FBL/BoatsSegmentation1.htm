Business Decisions

CreeqBoat, a boating company, had become a victim of the crisis in the boating industry. While exploring various growth options it became apparent to the management that in order to expand in different markets it lacked customer insight. In particular for North America, the management believes that in order to develop expansion strategies they need to be able to segment the current customer base. He wants to segment his customer base into 5-10 groups for marketing purposes, based on factor analysis carried out in the previous session. The manager has told us that he has a boat design for every customer, his problem at the moment is getting people in the door. He has a marketing budget to advertise in several different channels, with the opportunity to emphasise different messages in different channels. We expect that customers are attracted by different attributes in boats and also by different emotions related to boating. The aim of the segmentation is to refine the message of CreeqBoat's marketing to attract groups in the population most likely to buy their boats. We know that some customers will not be easily convinced to visit a boat shop by an advertising campaign so we will also select target clusters to whom it will be easiest to market. When describing the clusters we will focus on the characteristics which we can use to target marketing. This report describes the process and results of the initial customer segmentation.

The Data

Our input data consists of survey answers to a number of questions that relate to the type of boat that people might be interested in. Note that the respondents all have a boat: the survey therefore does not tell us anything about people who do not currently own a boat. The survey consists of 29 questions, each answered with an integer score between 1 and 5 inclusive.

We also have additional attribute and demographic data, which is also ranked on a metric scale but with different ranges of options. These attributes include information about current boat ownership, age and gender and income. Depending on the information the metric range is defined, e.g., gender is either 1 or 2, houshold income can range in categories between 1 and 19.

Our Approach

Clustering and Segmentation in 8 steps

  1. Confirm the data is metric

  2. Decide whether to scale or standardize the data

  3. Decide which variables to use for clustering

  4. Define similarity or dissimilarity measures between observations

  5. Visualize Individual Attributes and Pair-wise Distances between the Observations

  6. Select the clustering method to use and decide how many clusters to have

  7. Profile and interpret the clusters

  8. Assess the robustness of our clusters

Let's follow these steps.

Step 6. Select the clustering method to use and decide how many clusters to have

To answer the research question that we have posed, we have to derive a number of clusters of respondents who have provided answers to the questionnaire that are in some way related. It is important that the relationship of those answers is on dimensions that are relevant in answering our research question, which is about how to target the different customer segments. Intuitively, we would expect to end up with anywhere between five and ten segments. If there are less than five segments, it seems unlikely that most people within the segment share enough characteristics to allow us to reach them in a unified way. If there are more than ten segments, the number becomes so large that targeting each segment separately would be too much of an effort.

To arrive at the exact number of segments, we have experimented with a different numbers of clusters. Our decision finally depends on how well we are able to describe and target each segment in a distinctive way, while still allowing us limit the number of different segments that we have to adapt our marketing and sales approach for. Based on a number of iterations, we find that clustering in seven segments provides the balance that we are looking for.

In this note we will use two widely used methods: the Kmeans Clustering Method, and the Hierarchical Clustering Method. Like all clustering methods, these two also require that we have decided how to measure the distance/similarity between our observations. Explaining how these methods work is beyond our scope. The only difference to highlight is that Kmeans requires the user to define how many segments to create, while Hierarchical Clustering does not.

Let's fist use the Hierarchial Clustering method, as we do not know for now how many segments there are in our data. Hierarchical clustering is a method that also helps us visualise how the data may be clustering together. It generates a plot called the Dendrogram which is often helpful for visualization - but should be used with care. Step 7. Profile and interpret the clusters

In this step we are profiling and interpreting the clusters. After having seen in the previous step that we will select 7 clusters, our following analysis is concerned about the interpretation of these. In order to do so, we are using a two step approach. At first we will plot the average answers to the different questions by segment. Creating a snake plot of these different answers can already give us an indication whether our clusters are somewhat reasonable, i.e., whether we can see clear differences between the cluster lines of the snake plots. The second step of the analysis will identify the specific characteristics of the clusters. For this, we will plot each of the 81 questions and look at the average for each cluster to the average of the population. This will show us whether a specific cluster answers a question below or above average and will give us a deeper insight in how to characterize the customers allocated to this.

In our case, assuming we decided we use the 7 segments found using Kmeans as outlined above (similar profiling can be done with the results of hierarchical clustering or other segmentation methods), we can see how the responses to our survey differ across segments. Further analysis of the different segments with regards to their average responses to each of the question relative to the average response of the total population will give us further insight on the specific characteristics of the segment. However, before analyzing the data, we need to remind that the values stated in the following table only compare the averages relative to the average of the population. If we for example see a segment with a score of 1.3 in the gender question, it does not mean that the segment consists of more men than women (Men = 1, Women = 2) since we are comparing the averages where the ratio of men to women across all segments should be stated as 1. Also it doesn't mean, that more women than men are in this segment. It might be that the survey was answered by 20% women and 80% men (although this is not the case here). A value of 1.3 in this case would just indicate that the segment has a higher share of women than the average of the surveyed population.

This can also help us better understand whether there are indeed clusters in our data (e.g. if all segments are much like the overall population, there may be no segments). Intepretation, Decisions and Conclusion

We have followed a structured process to answer our initial research question, which is about how to tailor messages in different marketing channels to specific groups of customers. To answer this question, we have clustered boat owners in seven clearly distinct segments. Each of those segments has characteristics that allow us to target them in a tailored way:

Segment Approach
1. Single, tech-savvy women who view boats as a status symbolWe target this group through magazines that focus on general luxury products. This channel is not just focused on boats, but on other luxury products as well, which may allow us to use cross-selling or cross-advertising with for instance specific watch brands.
2. Brand loyal avid and passionate boaters who view boats as a status symbol and value powerful specifications; they tend to be price insensitive and employedWe target this group through magazines and websites that are dedicated to all aspects of boating life.
3. Tech-savvy boaters who do not see the boats as status symbol and do not view boating as primary hobbyWe expect investments in this group to be very limited, as their interest in boats in general is limited and the chance that they invest in our boats is likely to be even smaller. If we do anything to reach this group, it will be focused on their interest in technology, for instance through channels that focus on innovative technology-based accessories.
4. Passionate fishers who prefer simple boats, spend a lot of time boating alone and tend to repair boats themselves. They tend to be unemployed and do not view boats as status symbolsWe target this group through advertising at repair and maintenance shops. To appeal to this group, we may also invest in the visibility of our spare parts in such outlets.
5. Young employed avid boaters who view the boats as status symbols and prefer powerful boats. They tend to boat alone and are not likely to purchase in the futureWe expect to give limited attention to this segment. As this group is unlikely to buy new boats, the expected return on marketing investment is unlikely to be attractive.
6. Older customers who prefer basic boats, perform minor repairs themselves, boat rarely and do not see as a status symbol. Boat power does not matter for them and they are likely to purchase a boat in the future. We would target our more convenient boats to this segment, stressing the low-maintenance characteristics of our offerings. Relevant channels to reach these people likely include newspapers and general magazines. Further research in this group would be required before deciding to spend too much effort.
7. Unemployed older women who do not value technical characteristics and are not as avid as other customers in other segments. They are likely to purchase in the future and do not repair boats themselves. They are not likely to recommend the brand to other boaters and do not see a boat as a status symbolWe would target this group based on their appetite for convenience - low-thrills boats likely fit their interest. We would target this segment through magazines that are not just focused on boating and that are known to have an older, audience with a relatively high ratio of women.

We conclude that segments 1, 2 and 4 are most likely to be appealing to us, so our marketing investments would be focused on these segments. With the remaining budget, we would attempt to reach groups 6 and 7 as well. We are unlikely to invest in segments 3 and 5.
