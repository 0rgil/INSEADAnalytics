Business Decisions

Our team has segmented survey respondants into 7 different clusters, based mainly on 6 different factors. These factors, determined in the previous report, are:

  1. Boating as Leisure
  2. Boat Enthusiast
  3. Status Loving
  4. Brand Conscious
  5. Value Chasing
  6. Handy Man

The segments we came up with are as follows:
1. Unknowledgeable boater
2. Middle class
3. Luxury lover
4. Mr. Fix It
5. Muscle boater
6. Frustrated boater
7. Utility lover

This final segmentation was selected after performing several different analysis, using up to 10 clusters. The team found that, based both on the results and the graphical dendrogram, this was the best solution.

Our Approach

Clustering and Segmentation in 9 steps

  1. Confirm the data in metric

  2. Decide whether to scale or standardize the data

  3. Decide which variables to use for clustering

  4. Define similarity or dissimilarity measures between observations

  5. Visualize Individual Attributes and Pair-wise Distances between the Observations

  6. Select the clustering method to use and decide how many clusters to have

  7. Profile and interpret the clusters

  8. Assess the robustness of our clusters

Let's follow these steps.

Step 4. Define similarity or dissimilarity measures between observations

In our case we explore two distance metrics: the commonly used Euclidean distance as well as a simple one we define manually.

The Euclidean distance between two observations (in our case, customers) is simply the square root of the average of the square difference between the attributes of the two observations (in our case, customers). Let's now define our own distance metric, as an example. Let's say that the management team of the company believes that two customers are similar if they do not differ in their ratings of the attitudinal questions by more than 2 points. We can manually assign a distance of 1 for every question for which two customers gave an answer that differs by more than 2 points, and 0 otherwise. It is easy to write this distance function in R:

My_Distance_function <- function(x, y) {
    sum(abs(x - y) > 2)
}

Step 6. Select the clustering method to use and decide how many clusters to have

We tried using all the different clustering methods mentioned in the slideset, however decided to go with the Hierarchical Clustering with Euclidian measure.

Looking at the dendrogram, we tried many different number of clusters. We settled on 7 as it provided the cleanest visual cut, and the clusters were meaningful.

In this note we will use two widely used methods: the Kmeans Clustering Method, and the Hierarchical Clustering Method. Like all clustering methods, these two also require that we have decided how to measure the distance/similarity between our observations. Explaining how these methods work is beyond our scope. The only difference to highlight is that Kmeans requires the user to define how many segments to create, while Hierarchical Clustering does not.

Let's fist use the Hierarchial Clustering method, as we do not know for now how many segments there are in our data. Hierarchical clustering is a method that also helps us visualise how the data may be clustering together. It generates a plot called the Dendrogram which is often helpful for visualization - but should be used with care. For example, in this case the dendrogram, using the euclidean distance metric from the earlier steps and the ward hierarchical clustering option (see below as well as help(hclust) in R for more information), is as follows:

Note that we can draw as many clusters are we choose (e.g. in this case we chose 7 clusters) around the branches of the Dendrogram.

We can also plot the “distances” traveled before we need to merge any of the lower and smaller in size clusters into larger ones - the heights of the tree branches that link the clusters as we traverse the tree from its leaves to its root. If we have n observations, this plot has n-1 numbers.

Step 7. Profile and interpret the clusters

Our clusters were named as follows:

  1. The Unknowledgeable Boater is a boater who doesn???t not know a lot about boats or engines, is a slightly female oriented demographic, and is generally indifferent towards the brand, value, and quality.
  2. The Boat Expert is a boater who has the ability to perform and maintain his own boat, is not driven purely by price, but rather by brand and reputation, and is likely to recommend good brands to other buyers.

  3. The Boat Enthusiast is someone who takes boating very seriously and has based his or her entire lifestyle around boating. This boater knows his boats in-and-out, prefers take of the boat himself, is not price driven, spends A LOT of time on his boat, and views it as a status symbol. He is very likely to recommend our brand to people, however is not likely to purchase our brand himself, and will spend a lot more than average on a bigger and more powerful boat.

  4. The Value Seeking Boat Expert is similar to the regular boat expert, however is a lot more price sensitive and views price to be more important than the brand. This boater also prefers to maintain and perform minor repairs on his own boat.

  5. The Muscle Boater is a boat fanatic who spends a lot of time on his boat and is highly price driven, looking for the most powerful boat he can get on a limited budget. This demographic is significantly younger than the average boater and is more likely to use his or her boat for water sports.

  6. The Frustrated Boater is a boater who is not very knowledgeable about boats, is somewhat indifferent about the type of boat they prefer, and doesn???t not view boating as a very important part of his lifestyle. He or she has not had the best experiences of boating in the past however is likely to purchase our brand.

  7. The Utility Boater is a boater who owns a boat or plans to own a boat for practical or part time leisure purposes, and prefers to have a smaller engine. This boater is likely to take care of his own boat and is a slightly older demographic.
