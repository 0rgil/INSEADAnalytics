Our Approach

Clustering and Segmentation in 8 steps

  1. Confirm the data in metric

  2. Decide whether to scale or standardize the data

  3. Decide which variables to use for clustering

  4. Define similarity or dissimilarity measures between observations

  5. Visualize Individual Attributes and Pair-wise Distances between the Observations

  6. Select the clustering method to use and decide how many clusters to have

  7. Profile and interpret the clusters

  8. Assess the robustness of our clusters

Let's follow these steps.

We can manually assign a distance of 1 for every question for which two customers gave an answer that differs by more than 2 points, and 0 otherwise. It is easy to write this distance function in R:

My_Distance_function <- function(x, y) {

Step 6. Select the clustering method to use and decide how many clusters to have

In determining how many clusters to have, we will use two widely used methods: the Kmeans Clustering Method, and the Hierarchical Clustering Method. Both methods are similar in that they require that we have decided how to measure the distance/similarity between our observations. However, they differ in that Kmeans requires us to define how many segments to create, while Hierarchical Clustering does not.

The hierarchical method helps visualize clearly how many clusters there are and how they are grouped, by using the Dendrogram plot, as seen below:

In the Dendrogram, observations are grouped starting from pairs of individual observations which are the closest to each other, and merging smaller groups into larger ones depending on which groups are closest to each other. Eventually all our data is merged into one segment. The heights of the branches of the tree indicate how different the clusters merged at that level of the tree are. Longer lines indicate that the clusters below are very different. As expected, the heights of the tree branches increase as we traverse the tree from the end leaves to the tree root: the method merges data points/groups from the closest ones to the furthest ones.

The clusters can also be visualized with the scree plot. As a rule of thumb, one can select the number of clusters as the 'elbow' of this plot: this is the place in the tree where, if we traverse the tree from the leaves to its root, we need to make the 'longest jump' before we merge further the segments at that tree level. Of course the actual number of segments can be very different from what this rule of thumb may indicate. In practice, we explore different numbers of segments, possibly starting with what a hierarchical clustering dendrogram may indicate, and eventually we select the final segmentation solution using both statistical and qualitative criteria.

Step 7. Profile and interpret the clusters

Clustering comes out differently depending on the number of clusters used as well as the method of clustering. The goal is to seperate meaningfully different groups based on their responses to questions, but not to seperate into so many groups that each one is essentially the same as the next.

We identified names for the 5 segments and characteristics to describe them:

Segment 1.) Status Boater

  • The Status Boater cares about the brand of his/her boat, but is more strongly concerned about the technology and power of the boat.
  • The Status Boater considers him/herself passionate and knowledgeable about boating and reports boating more than other segments.
  • The Status Boater also sees the benefits of boating for other areas of life, including sport, adventure, socializing and relaxation
  • The Status boater is the least concerned with price of the identified segments and is the most affluent by a small margin
  • The Status Boater does not feel inclinded to purchase this brand of boat in the future, but is extremely likely to recommend it for someone else
  • The Status Boater is frequently unemployed

Segment 2.) Socialite

  • The Socialite tends to be a working woman.
  • To him/her a boat is about enteraining and status and he/she identifies less than average with all other uses of boats
  • He/she is extremely unlikely to repair a boat on his/her own and does not feel like a boating expert. He/she tends to rely on expert opinion when purchasing a boat, but not as strongly as some other segments
  • The brand of boat is very important when selecting a boat, and price is unimportant
  • He/she feels moderatly unlikely to purchase or recommend this boat brand in the future

Segment 3.) Handyman

  • The Handyman focuses on the functionality of the boat over brand or price when purchasing. He/she is not as interested in the power of the boat or the features or style.
  • The Handyman is older and more likely to be employed than the average person surveyed
  • Despite the fact that the Handyman considers him/herself likely to purchase this brand in the future, he/she does not feel likely to recommend this brand to others.
  • Lastly, the reason the Handyman is thus named is because he is likely to repair and maintain his own boat.

Segment 4.) Young Enthusiast

  • The Young Enthusiast is very concerned about price. Second to price is the power that the boat offers as well as the technology, but having many features is not important.
  • He/She enjoys boating and considers it his/her top free time activity and true passion in life and considers him/herself an expert.
  • The Young Enthusiast is a solo boater and ses his/her boat for sport.
  • The Young Enthusiast is younger than other segments and has slightly less money.
  • He/She feels unlikely to purchase this brand in the future but is very willing to recommend it to others

Segment 5.) Novice

  • The Novice is somewhat apathetic/negative about reasons to purchase a boat, perhaps because the group is not homogenous on this issue.
  • The Novice is slightly older, more employed and more often female than the average person surveyed
  • He/she relies on expert opinion when purchasing a boat and spend slightly less money on currently owned boats than average
  • The Novice is extremly unlikely to repair his/her own boat(s) and does not consider boating a passion.
  • The Novice is very unlikely to recommend this brand to others but feels likely to buy this brand in the future.
